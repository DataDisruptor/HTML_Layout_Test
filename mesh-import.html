Importing 3D Static Meshes to engine with optimal UV Texel Density

Ever wondered why does your 3d scene is lacking consistency?
Why does some static meshes you import from a DCC look sharp and crisp, while others are just dull and lacking in detail?
If you answered yes to both of these - then 99.9999% the culprit is Texel Density!

First, let's consider the fact, that while your screen uses pixels to display rgba data, a 3d object wrapped with an image must have some way of measuring the spread of pixels within a polygon.
It is this equivalent of a pixel we are thinking about here, which when measured and compared against, can equate to the pixels on our screen.
To be more precise, we need a way to keep track of how much pixels a texture renders by the engine - ON EVERY POLYGON - in a precise manner, that does not leaves any room for ambiguity.
By utilizing Texel Density application to our 3d meshes in our DCC, we earn the capability of being in control over how much detail each mesh is portrayed through.
With this new found degree of control, the precision in which we wrap the UV layout of our meshes will guarantee their coherence when all are rendered together in a 3d scene.

But how does Texel Density work?

Texels are measured in real-world units: this means, that even though a pixel is equal to a texel, we think of how much pixels we want to fit within 1 Meter in the scene.
For example, a Pixel Density of 10.24 would fit 1024 pixels within 1 meter in our scene.
A Pixel Density of 20.48 would fit 2048 pixels within 1 meter in our scene.
A Pixel Density of 40.96 would fit 4096 pixels within 1 meter in our scene.

Knowing this, we can decide how much detail objects should be given, based on our projects performance budget, as well as the placement of the objects in the 3d scene - in relation to the player's position.
It would not make any sense to give the underneath of a car that no one is going to see a 4K texture, nor will it make sense to give a distant mountain whose details could not be perceived from afar an 8K texture!
The same logic applies to texel density - if it's an object that is likely to be closer to our player viewpoint, it should be getting a higher Pixel Density standard.
Conversely, if it's an object that is likely to be further away from the player's viewpoint, then it should be getting a lower Pixel Density standard.

Texel Density to Meters
Texel Density Value Calculation Pixels per Meter
2.56 2.56 x 100 256px
5.12 5.12 x 100 512px
10.24 10.24 x 100 1,024px
20.48 20.48 x 100 2048px
40.96 40.96 x 100 4096px

Basically, the UV islands of our 3d mesh can be set with a TD value that determines the amount of detail it will have (according to a pixels-per-meter ratio), which would define a precise level of detail.
Now, this is just a one 3d model, which is a decent amount of work in of itself. Before ever being concerned about Texel Density, our mesh should have a proper UV layout wrapped - with no overlaps, unless each island represents a diferent Material Slot.
